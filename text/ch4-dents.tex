    This chapter considers MCTS algorithms for planning in single-objective environments, with the optional use of entropy as a secondary objective for exploration. The discussion focuses on the exploration setting for reinforcement learning (see Section \ref{sec:2-3-rl} and Figure \ref{fig:4:rl_overview}), where the agent is assessed purely on the recommendations it makes, rather than what actions it explored in simulation. 
    
    In maximum entropy reinforcement learning (Section \ref{sec:2-3-1-merl}), entropy is added directly to the objective function rather than used as a constraint to remove ambiguity between multiple optimal policies, as in inverse reinforcement learning \cite{max_ent_irl,max_ent_irl_thesis}. Whilst the maximum entropy objective yields a unique optimal policy, it is not guaranteed to align with the standard objective. This chapter demonstrates that the temperature parameter may have to be set to a prohibitively small value for the objectives to align, which in practice can negate the benefit of entropy-based exploration.

    This motivates the investigation of \entropyq in this chapter, and lays the foundations for \contextq\ewe in Chapter \ref{ch:6-simplexmaps}.

    The remainder of this chapter is organised as follows:
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Section \ref{sec:4-1-intro} discusses limitations of existing MCTS algorithms in the exploration setting of reinforcement learning. \textit{Simple regret} is defined in the context of sequential decision making, to theoretically analyse the performance of MCTS algorithms in the exploration setting.
        \item Section \ref{sec:4-2-boltzmannsearch} defines the \textit{Boltzmann Tree Search} (BTS) and \textit{Decaying ENtropy Tree Search} (DENTS) algorithms.
        \item Section \ref{sec:4-2-3-stoch_search_policies} discusses some properties of using a stochastic search policy in MCTS, and examines how the \textit{Alias Method} can be used to address \complexityq.
        \item Section \ref{sec:4-3-toyenvs} introduces two theoretical MDPs, which are used to empirically demonstrate the limitations of the existing MCTS algorithms.
        \item Section \ref{sec:4-4-results} presents experimental results on grid world environments and the game of Go, and gives an empirical answer to \entropyq.
        \item Section \ref{sec:4-5-theory} presents the main theoretical analysis and proofs. Convergence properties of MENTS, BTS and DENTS are proven to provide a theoretical answer to \entropyq.
    \end{itemize}

    This section considers a grid world shortest path problem, which is used to discuss the behaviour of UCT and MENTS in the exploration setting, and highlight some limitations of these algorithms.

    In this grid world, the agent starts at the origin $(0,0)$, and must reach the goal state at $(6,6)$. Each timestep, the agent may move deterministically in any cardinal direction, $\{\texttt{UP},\texttt{RIGHT},\texttt{DOWN},\texttt{LEFT}\}$, as long as it remains in the grid. Each action incurs a cost of 1 (or a reward of -1), so the cumulative cost of a path is equal to its length. The time horizon is chosen to be 36 (six times the width of the grid) so that the horizon is long enough for exploration. The optimal policy will always select from the actions \texttt{UP} and \texttt{RIGHT}. The problem, with example paths, is illustrated in Figure \ref{fig:4:shortest_path_intro}.

    While UCT and MENTS can easily solve this problem given enough trials and appropriately set parameters, it is used with a limited number of trials to illustrate the behaviour of the algorithms in the exploration setting. In Section \ref{sec:4-3-toyenvs}, MDPs are constructed adversarially to expose limitations of UCT and MENTS, and Section \ref{sec:4-4-results} considers more complex grid worlds --- including terminal states, sparse rewards and stochastic transition distributions --- that are harder to solve in practice.

\subsection{UCT}

    The UCT algorithm (Section \ref{sec:2-4-2-uct}) is designed in the traditional reinforcement learning setting described in Section \ref{sec:2-3-rl}, where the UCT agent aims to minimise the cumulative regret. Thus UCT makes a trade-off between exploration and exploitation during its trials, and frequently chooses the same action to exploit, which can result in it getting stuck in local optima, particularly when rewards are sparse or not informative. 

    A \textit{sparse reward} is where the reward signal is infrequent, that is, most actions give a reward of zero. In the shortest path example, the reward is dense but uninformative, as the immediate cost of taking any action is the same. 

    Suppose that UCT has been run for many trials, and the best paths found by UCT so far are shown in red and blue in Figure \ref{fig:4:shortest_path_intro_a}. The two paths coincide until the shaded state at $(2,2)$, where UCT has the Q-value estimates $\Quct((2,2),\texttt{DOWN}) \approx -8$ and $\Quct((2,2),\texttt{UP}) \approx -12$. When a UCT trial reaches the shaded state at $(2,2)$, it will frequently select the action \texttt{DOWN} over \texttt{UP} to exploit the best path it has found so far, rather than exploring the optimal actions \texttt{UP} and \texttt{RIGHT}.

    In theory, UCT selects every action infinitely often (that is, $N(s,a)\rightarrow \infty$ for all reachable $s,a$) and will converge to the optimal values and policy. In practice, UCT shifts its focus from exploration to exploitation throughout the search, which is determined by the confidence intervals and the bias parameter $\buct$ from Equation (\ref{eq:2:uct_policy}). This is illustrated in Figure \ref{fig:4:shortest_path_intro_results}, where for small bias parameters UCT quickly exploits a suboptimal solution and for large bias parameters UCT is still exploring and its value estimates have not yet converged.

\subsection{MENTS}

    One can argue that when using the maximum entropy objective, it is possible to recover the standard objective by setting the temperature parameter to zero, or an infinitesimally small value. However, in practice, setting $\alpha$ to a tiny value will nullify the exploration advantages of using entropy. Considering the other extreme, when a very large temperature is used, entropy becomes the dominant objective in the maximum entropy objective, and the optimal soft policy will be (almost) uniform. 

    The optimal soft policy for a large temperature may be different from the optimal policy for the standard objective. In cases such as this, it can be said that the maximum entropy objective is \textit{misaligned} with the standard objective. 
    \begin{defn} 
        The policy induced by the optimal soft Q-values is $\psi^*_{\sft}(s) = \argmax_{a'} Q^*_{\sft}(s,a')$. The maximum entropy objective is \textnormal{misaligned} when the policy $\psi^*_{\sft}(s) \neq \pi^*(s)$, where $\pi^*$ is the optimal standard policy (Definition \ref{def:2:opt_policy}). The \textnormal{misalignment threshold} is the smallest temperature parameter at which the maximum entropy objective is misaligned.
    \end{defn}

    In Figure \ref{fig:4:shortest_path_intro_b}, the optimal soft policy for the maximum entropy objective is shown for varying temperature parameters. For small temperature values ($\alpha<1$, illustrated in red), the optimal soft policy is close to the optimal standard policy, and for large temperature values ($\alpha>100$, illustrated in green) the optimal soft policy is nearly uniform, except at states adjacent to the goal, where the agent will avoid reaching the goal to obtain more entropy ``reward''. 

    When using the maximum entropy objective, the temperature parameter often needs to be carefully tuned, large enough to gain an exploration benefit, but small enough to avoid misalignment issues.  In this example problem, the misalignment threshold is around $\alpha=10$, as can be seen in Figure \ref{fig:4:shortest_path_intro_results_a}. Moreover, because the entropy of a policy depends on the MDP, the temperature typically needs to be tuned for each MDP. In Section \ref{sec:4-3-toyenvs}, an MDP is constructed where the misalignment threshold is prohibitively small for the maximum entropy objective to be effective, and Section \ref{sec:4-4-results} considers more complex grid worlds where the misalignment threshold is low enough to significantly affect performance.

\subsection{Simple Regret and Consistency}

    This section generalises the concept of simple regret (Definition \ref{def:2:simple_regret}) to MDPs and introduces the concept of consistency for MCTS algorithms. \textit{Simple regret} is used to motivate and analyse the algorithms introduced in this chapter. \textit{Consistency} refers to the correctness of an MCTS algorithm in the limit — informally, a consistent algorithm will eventually find the optimal policy given enough trials. The term originates from the MCTS literature, to describe whether modifications to UCT preserve its asymptotic behaviour \cite{consistency,mcts_survey,poly_uct}.

    \begin{figure}
        \begin{tcolorbox}
            Parameters: An MDP $\cl{M}$.
            \begin{itemize}
                \item For each round $m=1,2,\ldots$:
                \begin{enumerate}
                    \item the agent produces a search policy $\pi^m$ to follow;
                    \item the environment samples a trajectory $\tau\sim\pi^m$ (including rewards $r_t=R(s_t,a_t)$ for each $s_t,a_t$ pair in $\tau$);
                    \item the agent produces a recommendation policy $\psi^m$;
                    \item if the environment sends a stop signal, then the game ends, otherwise the next round starts.
                \end{enumerate} 
            \end{itemize}
        \end{tcolorbox}
        \caption[The procedure of an exploration planning problem for MDPs]{The procedure of an exploration planning problem for MDPs, where $\psi^m$ is the recommendation policy the agent produces after $m$ trajectories are sampled using the exploration policy $\pi^m$.}
        \label{fig:4:planning_problem}
    \end{figure}

    The \textit{simple regret} of a policy is the difference between the value of the policy and optimal value of the policy (Equation (\ref{eq:4:simple_regret})). Whilst \textit{cumulative regret} is used to analyse algorithms in the standard reinforcement learning setting, simple regret is more appropriate for the exploration setting, where the agent is assessed only on its recommendations and not penalised for exploring poor actions in simulation.

    \begin{defn}
        \label{def:4:simple_regret}
        The \textnormal{simple regret} of a policy $\psi$ at state $s\in\cl{S}$ is the difference between the value of the policy and the optimal value at that state:
        \begin{align}
            \sreg(s,\psi) = V^*(s)-V^{\psi}(s). \label{eq:4:simple_regret}
        \end{align}
    \end{defn}

    \todo{make sure simple regret is in the table of notation}

    By definition, the optimal policy achieves a simple regret of zero. In this thesis an MCTS algorithm is considered \textit{consistent} if its expected simple regret tends to zero. Informally, a consistent algorithm will eventually output an optimal policy if run indefinitely, and is expected to improve its recommendation with more trials.

    \begin{defn}
        \label{def:4:consistency}
        An agent, that produces recommendation policies $\psi^1,\psi^2,\ldots$ is said to be \textnormal{consistent} if $\bb{E}[\sreg(s,\psi^m)] \rightarrow 0$ as $m\rightarrow \infty$.
    \end{defn}

    Note that the simple regret (Equation (\ref{eq:4:simple_regret})) is a random variable, as it depends on the recommendation policy $\psi$, which itself depends on the random trajectories that are sampled. Hence the expected simple regret is used in Definition \ref{def:4:consistency} to define consistency, and is the main value of interest in the theoretical analysis in Section \ref{sec:4-5-theory}.

    \citet{explore_bandit} show that upper bounds on cumulative regret lead to both upper and lower bounds on simple regret. Returning to the discussion around UCT and MENTS, using this result, UCT is consistent with respect to Definition \ref{def:4:consistency} because its upper bound on cumulative regret \cite{uct_long} leads to an upper bound on simple regret that tends to zero. However, MENTS is only consistent for a sufficiently small temperature parameter that depends on the MDP (\todo{ref section and/or theorem later in chapter}).

\section{Boltzmann Search}
\label{sec:4-2-boltzmannsearch}
    
    This section defines the new algorithms Boltzmann Tree Search (BTS) and Decaying ENtropy Tree Search (DENTS). Following the discussion from Section \ref{sec:4-1-intro}, the design aims of these algorithms are as follows:
    \begin{itemize}
        \item to be as simple to define and implement as UCT and MENTS\footnote{i.e. each decision node operates on a multi-armed bandit problem};
        \item to be able to explore effectively when rewards are sparse or uninformative;
        \item to be able to exploit when necessary, in large or stochastic domains;
        \item to be consistent using parameters that do not depend on the MDP.
    \end{itemize}

    In Section \ref{sec:4-2-1-bts} BTS is defined, which uses a Boltzmann search policy, similar to MENTS, but using the standard objective and Bellman backups rather than the soft Bellman backups used in MENTS. Section \ref{sec:4-2-2-dents} defines DENTS which re-introduces entropy as an exploration bonus while maintaining consistency. Section \ref{sec:4-2-3-stoch_search_policies} discusses the use of stochastic search policies in MCTS further, highlighting that mixture policies can be used to incorporate prior knowledge from $\piinit$ (Section \ref{sec:4-2-3-1-mixture_policies}) and how the \textit{Alias Method} can be used to arrive at a more efficient MCTS algorithm addressing \complexityq\ewe (Section \ref{sec:4-2-3-2-alias_method}).

\subsection{Boltzmann Tree Search}
\label{sec:4-2-1-bts}

    \paragraph{Algorithm Definition}

    \todo{search for Boltzmann Tree Search and BTS, and check that were not claiming to invent it. Add somewhere cites to RTDP and THTS which have also used this.     The \textit{Boltzmann Tree Search} (BTS) algorithm uses the variables $\Vbts$ and $\Qbts$ for value estimates, $N(s)$ and $N(s,a)$ for visit counts, and the function $\alphabts(x)>0$ for the search temperature. The temperature used at each decision node $s$ is computed with respect to the visit count $N(s)$ as $\alphabts(N(s))$.

    The BTS search policy uses the flag $\normalisedq$ to optionally normalise Q-values, and incorporates prior knowledge from $\Qinit$ weighted by $\Nqbts$ pseudo-visits:
    %
    \begin{align}
        \Qbtsbias(s,a) &= \frac{N(s,a) \Qbts(s,a) +  \Nqbts \Qinit(s,a)}{N(s,a)+\Nqbts}, \\
        \Qbtsnorm(s,a) &= 
        \begin{cases}
            \frac{\Qbtsbias(s,a)-\min_{a\in\cl{A}}\Qbtsbias(s,a)}{\max_{a\in\cl{A}}\Qbtsbias(s,a)-\min_{a\in\cl{A}}\Qbtsbias(s,a)} & \text{if } \normalisedq, \\
            \Qbtsbias(s,a) & \text{otherwise.}
        \end{cases}
    \end{align}

    The search policy of BTS is similar to MENTS (Section \ref{sec:2-4-4-ments}), but using a variable temperature and (the normalised and biased) Bellman value estimates rather than soft value estimates.
    %
    \begin{align}
        \pibts(a|s) &= (1-\lambdabts)\rhobts(a|s) + \frac{\lambdabts}{|\cl{A}|}, 
                    \label{eq:4:bts_search_policy} \\ 
        \rhobts(a|s) &\propto \exp\left(\frac{\Qbtsnorm(s,a)}{\alphabts(N(s))}\right),
                    \label{eq:4:bts_value_policy} \\
        \lambda(s,\epsilon) &= \min\left(1, \frac{\epsilon}{\log(e+N(s))}\right), \label{eq:4:lambda}
    \end{align}
    %
    where $\epsbts \in [0,\infty)$ is an exploration parameter. 

    For any new decision nodes $s$ added to the tree, the $\initv$ function initialises the visit count to $N(s)=0$ and the value estimate to $\Vbts(s)=\Vinit(s)$. For any new chance nodes $(s,a)$ added to the tree, the $\initq$ function initialises the visit count to $N(s,a)=0$ and the Q-value estimate to $\Qbts(s,a)=\Qinit(s,a)$.
    
    BTS also allows the value estimates to be biased by $\Vinit$ with a weighting of $\Nvbts$ pseudo-visits. When \mctsmode\ewe is True, $\Nvbts$ is typically set to one, weighting the heuristic value estimate as a single pseudo-trial. When \mctsmode\ewe is False, $\Nvbts$ is set to zero, as the trajectory already captures the rollout information.
    
    Let $\tau_{:h}=(s_0,a_0,r_0,\ldots,s_{h-1},a_{h-1},r_{h-1},s_h)\sim\pibts$ be the trajectory sampled during the selection phase. The $\backupv$ and $\backupq$ functions then update the value estimates for $t=h-1,\ldots,0$:
    \begin{align}
        \Qbts(s_t,a_t) &\leftarrow 
            R(s_t,a_t) + \sum_{s' \in \suc{s_t}{a_t}} \left( \frac{N(s')}{N(s_t,a_t)} \Vbts(s') \right), 
                    \label{eq:4:bts_backup_q} \\ 
        \Vbts(s_t) &\leftarrow \frac{\Nvbts \Vinit(s_t) + N(s_t) \max_{a\in\cl{A}} \Qbts(s_t,a)}{N(s_t)+\Nvbts}.
                    \label{eq:4:bts_backup_v} 
    \end{align}
    
    When BTS needs to recommend a policy, it can use its Q-value estimates, or the visit counts:
    %
    \begin{align}
        \psibts(s)=\argmax_{a\in\cl{A}}\Qbts(s,a), \label{eq:4:psibts} \\
        \mvbts(s) = \argmax_{a\in\cl{A}} N(s,a). \label{eq:4:mvbts}
    \end{align}




    
    \paragraph{Average Returns}

    The BTS search policy can still be used with average returns. In \textit{Boltzmann Tree Search with Average Returns} (AR-BTS) the Bellman value estimates are replaced with average returns $\Varbts$ and $\Qarbts$. Given a trajectory $\tau=(s_0,a_0,r_0,\ldots,s_{h-1},a_{h-1},r_{h-1},s_h)$ the average returns are updated for $t=h-1,\ldots,0$:
    \begin{align}
        \Qarbts(s_t,a_t) &\leftarrow 
            \frac{N(s_t,a_t)-1}{N(s_t,a_t)} \left( \Qarbts(s_t,a_t) 
                + \frac{\Vh(s_h) + \sum_{i=t}^{h-1} r_i}{N(s_t,a_t)-1} \right), \label{eq:4:arbsts_backup_q} \\
        \Varbts(s_t) &\leftarrow 
            \frac{N(s_t)-1}{N(s_t)} \left( \Varbts(s_t) 
                + \frac{\Vh(s_h) + \sum_{i=t}^{h-1} r_i}{N(s_t)-1} \right), \label{eq:4:arbsts_backup_v} 
    \end{align}
    %
    where $\Vh$ is a shorthand for $\Vinit$.

    The corresponding definitions of $\Qarbtsnorm$, $\piarbts$, $\psiarbts$, $\mvbts$ and so on are similar to the definitions for BTS and are omitted for brevity. The complete equations for all algorithms can be found in Appendix \ref{app:ar-algs} \todo{double check this ref}.

    In Section \ref{sec:4-5-theory} it is shown that the BTS recommendation policy will eventually converge to the optimal policy.
    \begin{restatable}{theorem}{thrmBTS} 
        \label{thrm:4:bts} 
        For any MDP $\cl{M}$, if $\epsbts > 0$, the expected simple regret of $\psibts$ tends to zero: $\bb{E}[\sreg(s_0,\psibts)]\rightarrow 0$.
    \end{restatable}

    Additionally, if the temperature function is allowed to tend to zero, then the search policy will also converge to the optimal policy, and the most visited recommendation policy will also converge to the optimal policy as a result. 
    \begin{restatable}{theorem}{thrmBTSdecay} \label{thrm:4:bts_decay}
        For any MDP $\cl{M}$, if $\epsbts > 0$ and $\alphabts(x)\rightarrow 0$, then $\pibts\rap \pi^*$ and $\bb{E}[\sreg(s_0,\mvbts)]\rightarrow 0$.
    \end{restatable}

    Conversely, if the temperature has a strictly positive lower bound (and does not converge to zero), then the BTS recommendation policy will converge to the optimal policy exponentially fast. However, note that neither the search policy nor the most-visited recommendation policy is guaranteed to converge to the optimal policy in this case.
    \begin{restatable}{theorem}{thrmBTSexp} \label{thrm:4:bts_exp}
        For any MDP $\cl{M}$, if there exists $\alphabts(x)\geq L > 0$, then there exist constants $C,k>0$ such that $\bb{E}[\sreg(s_0,\psibts)] \leq C\exp(-kn)$ after running $n$ trials of BTS.
    \end{restatable}

    For AR-BTS, the temperature function always needs to be restricted to functions that tend to zero. Intuitively, the average return estimates converge only if the search policy converges, and the search policy converges only if the temperature tends to zero.
    \begin{restatable}{theorem}{thrmARBTS} \label{thrm:4:ar_bts}
        For any MDP $\cl{M}$, if $\epsarbts > 0$ and $\alphaarbts(x)\rightarrow 0$, then $\piarbts\rap \pi^*$, $\bb{E}[\sreg(s_0,\psiarbts)]\rightarrow 0$ and  $\bb{E}[\sreg(s_0,\mvarbts)]\rightarrow 0$.
    \end{restatable}

\subsection{Decaying ENtropy Tree Search}
\label{sec:4-2-2-dents}

    \paragraph{Algorithm Definition}

    \textit{Decaying ENtropy Tree Search} (DENTS) extends the BTS algorithm by adding \textit{entropy estimates} as an exploration bonus. DENTS uses Bellman value estimates $\Vdents$ and $\Qdents$ similarly to BTS, but also uses entropy value estimates, $\HVdents$ and $\HQdents$, which are Monte Carlo estimates of the entropy of the search policy rooted from the relevant node. By keeping a separate estimate for entropy, DENTS is able to use entropy in its search policy, and discard it for recommendations.

    DENTS uses parameters that correspond to the BTS parameters: $\normalisedq$, $\Nqdents$, $\Nvdents$, $\alphadents(x)>0$ and $\epsdents$. Additionally, the entropy terms are weighted by a non-negative function $\betadents(x)\geq 0$ during the selection phase. Typically, $\betadents$ is chosen such that $\betadents(x)\rightarrow 0$ as $x\rightarrow\infty$, so that the entropy weighting diminishes over time, allowing deeper parts of the tree to be explored\footnote{In practice, it is also advisable to choose $\betadents$ such that $\frac{\betadents(x)}{\alphadents(x)}\rightarrow 0$, so that the entropy weighting decays faster than the temperature. Otherwise, even as the temperature vanishes, the entropy bonus may still dominate the Q-values in the Boltzmann exponent, skewing the search policy away from the optimal action.}.
    
    The Q-values used during search are computed as follows:
    \begin{align}
        \Qdentssoft(s,a) &= \Qdents(s,a)+\betadents(N(s))\HQdents(s,a), \\
        \Qdentsbias(s,a) &= \frac{N(s,a) \Qdentssoft(s,a) +  \Nqdents \Qinit(s,a)}{N(s,a)+\Nqdents}, \\
        \Qdentsnorm(s,a) &= 
        \begin{cases}
            \frac{\Qdentsbias(s,a)-\min_{a\in\cl{A}}\Qdentsbias(s,a)}{\max_{a\in\cl{A}}\Qdentsbias(s,a)-\min_{a\in\cl{A}}\Qdentsbias(s,a)} & \text{if } \normalisedq, \\
            \Qdentsbias(s,a) & \text{otherwise.}
        \end{cases}
    \end{align}

    The DENTS search policy is similar to BTS: 
    %
    \begin{align}
        \pidents(a|s) &= (1-\lambdadents)\rhodents(a|s) + \frac{\lambdadents}{|\cl{A}|}, 
                    \label{eq:dents_search_policy} \\ 
        \rhodents(a|s) &\propto \exp\left(\frac{\Qdentsnorm(s,a)}{\alphadents(N(s))}\right),
                    \label{eq:dents_value_policy} \\
        \lambda(s,\epsilon) &= \min\left(1, \frac{\epsilon}{\log(e+N(s))}\right).
    \end{align}

    The $\initv$ and $\initq$ functions are also similar to BTS, setting: $N(s)=0$, $N(s,a)=0$, $\Vdents(s)=\Vinit(s)$ and $\Qdents(s,a)=\Qinit(s,a)$. Additionally, the entropy value estimates are initialised to zero: $\HVdents(s)=0$ and $\HQdents(s,a)=0$.

    Given a trajectory $\tau=(s_0,a_0,r_0,\ldots,s_{h-1},a_{h-1},r_{h-1},s_h)\sim\pidents$, the $\backupq$ and $\backupv$ functions update the entropy value estimates for $t=h-1,\ldots,0$:
    \begin{align}
        \HQdents(s_t,a_t) &\leftarrow \sum_{s'\in \suc{s_t}{a_t}} \frac{N(s')}{N(s_t,a_t)} \HVdents(s'), 
            \label{eq::4dents_entropy_q_backup} \\
        \HVdents(s_t) &\leftarrow \cl{H}(\pidents(\cdot | s_t)) + \sum_{a\in\cl{A}} \pidents(a|s_t)\HQdents(s_t,a), 
            \label{eq:4:dents_entropy_v_backup} 
    \end{align}
    %
    where $\cl{H}$ is the Shannon entropy function. The Bellman value estimates are updated similarly to BTS, for $t=h-1,\ldots,0$:
    \begin{align}
        \Qdents(s_t,a_t) &\leftarrow 
            R(s_t,a_t) + \sum_{s' \in \suc{s_t}{a_t}} \left( \frac{N(s')}{N(s_t,a_t)} \Vdents(s') \right), 
                    \label{eq:dents_q_backup} \\ 
        \Vdents(s_t) &\leftarrow \frac{\Nvdents \Vinit(s_t) + N(s_t) \max_{a\in\cl{A}} \Qdents(s_t,a)}{N(s_t)+\Nvdents}.
                    \label{eq:4:dents_backup_v} 
    \end{align}

    The recommendation policies for DENTS are also defined identically to BTS:
    \begin{align}
        \psidents(s) = \argmax_{a\in\cl{A}}\Qdents(s,a), \label{eq:4:psidents} \\
        \mvdents(s) = \argmax_{a\in\cl{A}} N(s,a), \label{eq:4:mvdents}
    \end{align}




    
    \paragraph{Average Returns}

    In \textit{Decaying ENtropy Tree Search with Average Returns} (AR-DENTS) the Bellman value estimates are replaced with average returns $\Vardents$ and $\Qardents$. For brevity and to avoid repetition, the equations are omitted but can be found in Appendix \ref{app:ar-algs} \todo{double check this ref}.
    






    \paragraph{Summary of Theoretical Results}

    Corresponding theoretical results hold for DENTS and AR-DENTS to those stated for BTS. It is additionally required that $\betadents\rightarrow 0$, so that the entropy bonus vanishes and the search policy is not skewed away from the optimal action. Proofs of the theorems below are given in Section \ref{sec:4-5-theory}.

    \begin{restatable}{theorem}{thrmDENTS} \label{thrm:4:dents}
        For any MDP $\cl{M}$, if $\epsdents > 0$, the expected simple regret of $\psidents$ tends to zero: $\bb{E}[\sreg(s_0,\psidents)]\rightarrow 0$.
    \end{restatable}

    \begin{restatable}{theorem}{thrmDENTSdecay} \label{thrm:4:dents_decay}
        For any MDP $\cl{M}$, if $\epsdents > 0$, $\alphadents(x)\rightarrow 0$ and $\betadents(x)\rightarrow 0$, then $\pidents\rap \pi^*$ and $\bb{E}[\sreg(s_0,\mvdents)]\rightarrow 0$.
    \end{restatable}

    \begin{restatable}{theorem}{thrmDENTSexp} \label{thrm:4:dents_exp}
        For any MDP $\cl{M}$, if there exists $\alphadents(x)\geq L > 0$ and $U\geq\betadents(x)$, then there exist constants $C,k>0$ such that $\bb{E}[\sreg(s_0,\psidents)] \leq C\exp(-kn)$ after running $n$ trials of DENTS.
    \end{restatable}
    
    \begin{restatable}{theorem}{thrmARDENTS} \label{thrm:4:ar_dents}
        For any MDP $\cl{M}$, if $\epsardents > 0$, $\alphaardents(x)\rightarrow 0$ and $\betaardents(x)\rightarrow 0$, then $\piardents\rap \pi^*$, $\bb{E}[\sreg(s_0,\psiardents)]\rightarrow 0$ and  $\bb{E}[\sreg(s_0,\mvardents)]\rightarrow 0$.
    \end{restatable}

\subsubsection{Incorporating Prior Knowledge Through Mixture Policies}
\label{sec:4-2-3-1-mixture_policies}

    \newcommand{\pimix}{{\pi_{\text{mix}}}}

    In MENTS, BTS and DENTS, a uniform distribution is mixed with the Boltzmann distribution to form the search policy, using a logarithmically decaying weight that gradually transitions towards the Boltzmann distribution as the number of visits increases. The prior policy $\piinit$ can be incorporated in the same way: by mixing it into the algorithm's search policy $\pialg$ with a decaying weight, prior knowledge guides exploration early in the search while leaving the asymptotic behaviour unchanged.

    The mixture policy $\pimix$ is defined by:
    \begin{align}
        \pi_{\text{mix}}(a|s) &= 
            (1-\lambda(s,\epsilon_{\text{mix}})) \pialg(a|s) 
            + \lambda(s,\epsilon_{\text{mix}}) \piinit(a|s),\\
        \lambda(s,\epsilon) &= \min\left(1, \frac{\epsilon}{\log(e+N(s))}\right).
    \end{align}
    %
    where $\epsilon_{\text{mix}}\in[0,\infty)$ controls the weight given to the prior policy in the mixture. The weight given to $\piinit$ decays with the visit count, eventually reaching zero, so that the behaviour of the algorithm is unchanged in the limit.

\subsubsection{A More Efficient MCTS}
\label{sec:4-2-3-2-alias_method}

    \newcommand{\old}{{\text{prev}}}
    \newcommand{\Qcorrected}{{S}}

    The discussion in this section assumes that $\mctsmode$ is true, and that $\graphmode$ is false. Additionally, the runtime complexities of $\initq$ and $\sampleoutcome$ are assumed to be $O(1)$\footnote{This is typically the case when using a simulator. If given an arbitrary MDP, the Alias Method can be used with some preprocessing cost to sample outcomes in $O(1)$ time.}. In this case, the runtime of one MCTS trial consists of the following time costs:
    \begin{description}
        \item[Selection Phase:] 
            The $\selectaction$ function is called at up to $H_{\thtspp}$ many decision nodes.
        \item[Heuristic Step:] 
            The $\initv$ function is called once to initialise the value of the new decision node.
        \item[Backup Phase:]
            The $\backupv$ and $\backupq$ functions are called at up to $H_{\thtspp}$ many decision nodes and chance nodes.
    \end{description}

    Hence, the worst case complexity of an MCTS trial in $\thtspp$ is:
    \begin{align}
        O(H_{\thtspp}\cdot \left(O(\selectaction)+O(\backupv)+O(\backupq)\right)+O(\initv)). \label{eq:4:mcts_trial_complexity}
    \end{align}

    When a rollout is used in $\initv$, its complexity is $O(H_{\thtspp}\cdot A)$, where $A=|\cl{A}|$ is the number of actions. Unfortunately this is the dominating term in the runtime complexity of an MCTS trial. However, when using function approximation in $\initv$, i.e. $\Vinit=\tilde{V}$, and $\piinit=\tilde{\pi}$, the runtime complexity of $\initv$ is $O(A)$, as $\piinit$ is evaluated for each action in $\cl{A}$ and the results cached. The rest of this subsection will assume that $\initv$ runs in $O(A)$ time using function approximation.

    Otherwise, $\selectaction$ is typically the dominating factor in Equation (\ref{eq:4:mcts_trial_complexity}), taking $O(A)$ time. For example in UCT (Section \ref{sec:2-4-2-uct}), the $\selectaction$ function must compute a UCB score for each action in $\cl{A}$, taking $O(A)$ time.

    However, if one is using a stochastic search policy and willing to use a slightly outdated search policy during selection, the Alias Method can be used as follows:
    \begin{itemize}
        \item In $\selectaction$, actions are sampled from an alias table stored in the decision node;
        \item In $\initv$, an initial alias table is constructed, with an additional $O(A)$ preprocessing cost, maintaining the $O(A)$ time complexity;
        \item In $\backupv$, the alias table is updated every $A$ visits to the decision node, with an additional $O(A)$ time cost every $A$ visits, giving only an amortised $O(1)$ time complexity on top of any backup equations used in $\backupv$.
    \end{itemize}

    The only remaining significant time costs are in $\backupv$ and $\backupq$. 

    \paragraph{Average Return Backups.}
    
    It is well known that the incremental updates for computing the average returns used in UCT (Equations (\ref{eq:2:uct_backup_q}) and (\ref{eq:2:uct_backup_v})), AR-BTS and AR-DENTS have a constant $O(1)$ complexity.

    \paragraph{Bellman $\backupv$.}

    In BTS and DENTS, the $\backupv$ function (Equations (\ref{eq:4:bts_backup_v}) and (\ref{eq:4:dents_backup_v})) computes a maximum over actions in $\cl{A}$. Because the Q-value of only one action $a_t$ is changed between the selection and backup phases, the maximum can be computed more efficiently using a max heap. $\backupv$ updates the Q-value\footnote{Note that in an arbitrary MDP, the Q-value may decrease due to stochasticity in the MDP. If the MDP is deterministic, computing the Q-values will only increase, and the maximum can be computed in $O(1)$ time.} corresponding to $a_t$ in the max heap, in $O(\log(A))$ time, and then the maximum value can be read from the top of the heap.
    
    \paragraph{Bellman $\backupq$.}
    
    In the algorithms considered in this thesis so far, the Q-value backups are all either Bellman or average return backups. Bellman Q-value backups can be computed incrementally in $O(1)$ time. Consider the general form of the Bellman update at chance node $(s_t,a_t)$, using the value estimates $\hat{V}$ and $\hat{Q}$:
    \begin{align}
        \hat{Q}(s_t,a_t) &\leftarrow 
            R(s_t,a_t) + \sum_{s' \in \suc{s_t}{a_t}} \left( \frac{N(s')}{N(s_t,a_t)} \hat{V}(s') \right). 
                \label{eq:4:local1} 
    \end{align}

    Because the value of only one child node, $s_{t+1}$, is changed between the selection and backup phases, Equation (\ref{eq:4:local1}) can be computed incrementally in $O(1)$ time as:
    \begin{align}
        \hat{Q}(s_t,a_t) &\leftarrow \begin{cases}
            R(s_t,a_t) + \hat{V}(s_{t+1}) & \text{ if } N(s_t,a_t) = 1, \\
            R(s_t,a_t)  
                + \Qcorrected(s_t,a_t)
                + \frac{N(s_{t+1})}{N(s_t,a_t)}\hat{V}(s_{t+1}) & \text{ otherwise,}
        \end{cases} \label{eq:4:local2} \\
        \Qcorrected(s_t,a_t) &= 
            \frac{N^{\old}(s_t,a_t)}{N(s_t,a_t)} \left( Q(s_t,a_t) - R(s_t,a_t) 
                - \frac{N^{\old}(s_{t+1})}{N^{\old}(s_t,a_t)} \hat{V}^{\old}(s_{t+1}) \right),
    \end{align}
    %
    where $S$ computes the corrected average of $\hat{V}(s')$ for $s'\in\suc{s_t}{a_t}-\{s_{t+1}\}$, $\hat{V}^{\old}(s_{t+1})$ is the previous value of $\hat{V}(s_{t+1})$, and $N^{\old}(s_{t+1}) = N(s_{t+1})-1$, $N^{\old}(s_t,a_t) = N(s_t,a_t)-1$ are the previous values of $N(s_{t+1})$, $N(s_t,a_t)$.

    \paragraph{MENTS $\backupv$.}

    Using the same observation as in the previous paragraph, the $\backupv$ function in MENTS (Equation (\ref{eq:2:ments_backup_v})) can be computed in $O(1)$ time. However, this is difficult to implement in a numerically stable way, because the \textit{correction term}\footnote{e.g. $\Qcorrected(s_t,a_t)$ in Equation (\ref{eq:4:local2})} involves products of exponentials.

    \paragraph{DENTS Entropy $\backupv$.}

    When the Alias Method is used with DENTS, the search policy $\pidents$ is updated every $A$ visits. On trials where the search policy is not updated, the same observation can be used again, correcting the value estimates using only the updated value of the child node whose value changed.

    When the Alias Method is not used, the search policy $\pidents$ may change on each trial, so Equation (\ref{eq:4:dents_entropy_v_backup}) needs to be recomputed, taking $O(A)$ time.

    \paragraph{Summary of Complexities.}

    The complexities of the different components of an MCTS trial are summarised in Table \ref{table:mcts_trial_complexity}. To answer \complexityq in the affirmative, when the Alias Method is used with the most efficient implementations of $\backupv$ and $\backupq$, the complexity of running a trial of MENTS can be improved to $O(H_{\thtspp}+A)$. The complexity of running a trial of BTS and DENTS can be improved to $O(H_{\thtspp}\log(A)+A)$, and the complexity of running a trial of AR-BTS and AR-DENTS can be improved to $O(H_{\thtspp}+A)$.

\section{Theoretical MDPs}
\label{sec:4-3-toyenvs}

    This section builds on the discussion from Section \ref{sec:4-1-intro} to construct adversarial MDPs that expose the limitations of UCT and the maximum entropy objective.
    
    \citet{dchain} introduce the \textit{D-chain problem}, which is a theoretical MDP for which UCT requires exponentially many trials to find the optimal policy. MENTS is able to solve the problem in a small number of trials due to its maximum entropy objective. However, Section \ref{sec:4-3-1-dchain} demonstrates that changing a single reward in the MDP can shift the misalignment threshold to a prohibitively small value, rendering the maximum entropy objective ineffective.
    
    In Section \ref{sec:4-3-2-entropy_trap}, the D-chain problem is extended to include an \textit{entropy trap}, to create an MDP for which both UCT and MENTS cannot solve in any feasible time.

\subsection{The D-Chain Problem}
\label{sec:4-3-1-dchain}

    \newcommand{\aL}{a^L_{\cl{M}}}
    \newcommand{\aR}{a^R_{\cl{M}}}
    \newcommand{\schain}[1]{s^{#1}_{\cl{M}}}

    \begin{figure}
        \centering
        \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figures/ch4/mod_dchain_mdp.pdf}
        \caption[An illustration of the \textit{(modified) D-chain problem}.]{An illustration of the \textit{(modified) D-chain problem}, where $\schain{1}$ is the starting state and transitions are deterministic. The action $\aL$ from state $\schain{i}$ results in an immediate reward of $\frac{D-i}{D}$ and the trial ends. Taking action $\aR$ to the end of the chain results in a reward of $R_f$. Where $R_f=1$ in the original D-chain, and $R_f=\frac{1}{2}$ in the modified D-chain.}
        \label{fig:modified_d_chain}
    \end{figure}

    The \textit{D-chain problem} consists of a chain of $D$ states with two possible actions and is depicted in Figure \ref{fig:modified_d_chain}. If the action $\aL$ is taken from state $\schain{i}$, then an immediate reward of $R(\schain{i},\aL)=\frac{D-i}{D}$ is collected and the trial ends. If the action $\aR$ is taken the agent continues along the chain without any reward, until the end of the chain is reached where the agent receives a reward of $R(\schain{D},\aR)=1$. By inspection, the optimal policy is $\pi^*(s)=\aR$, to follow the chain to the end and collect the maximum reward of one.

    To intuitively understand why UCT will struggle on this MDP, the reward function can be decomposed into a dense and sparse component:
    \begin{align}
        R_{\dense}(\schain{i},\aL) &= \frac{D-i}{D}, \\
        R_{\dense}(\schain{i},\aR) &= 0, \\
        R_{\sparse}(\schain{i},a) &= \one[i=D] \cdot \one[a=\aR], \\
        R(s,a) &= R_{\dense}(s,a) + R_{\sparse}(s,a). \label{eq:4:local3}
    \end{align}

    The two components of the rewards can be seen to encourage the agent to perform two different behaviours. Intuitively, $R_{\dense}$ encourages the agent to leave the chain as soon as possible, while $R_{\sparse}$ rewards the agent for staying on the chain until the end. Since UCT balances exploration and exploitation, it will frequently select $\aL$ to exploit the immediate reward of $\frac{D-1}{D}$, rather than exploring to find the reward of one.

    Compounding this, once a trial of UCT reaches the unit reward for the first time, the value estimates of $\Vuct(\schain{i})$ still need many more trials to converge and recommend the optimal policy. This phenomenon can be seen in Figure \ref{fig:d_chain_results}, where UCT never recommends the optimal policy, while MaxUCT (\todo{add ref to ch2/3}) that uses the same search policy with Bellman backups will recommend the optimal policy once one trial reaches the unit reward at the end of the chain. 
    
    \citet{dchain} formally prove that UCT will suffer a regret of $\Omega((\exp \circ \exp \circ \ldots \circ \exp)(1))$, where $\circ$ denotes functional composition, and there are $D$ many composed exponential functions. 

    From the perspective of practical single-objective reinforcement learning, this issue may appear to be one of poor reward shaping. However, rewards with conflicting objectives similar to this naturally arise in the context of multi-objective reinforcement learning. An example of this will be seen in the \textit{Deep Sea Treasure} problem in Chapter \ref{ch:5-chmcts}, where the scalarised reward combines a dense time cost with a sparse treasure reward, yielding a structure similar to Equation (\ref{eq:4:local3}).

    Introducing entropy as an exploration bonus naturally encourages the agent to explore along the chain, because once the agent leaves the chain it can no longer collect a bonus entropy reward. As can be seen in Figure \ref{fig:d_chain_results}, the D-chain problem is an example of an MDP where the standard and maximum-entropy objectives are well aligned.

    To illustrate that in maximum entropy methods the temperature parameter requires careful per-MDP tuning, consider the \textit{modified D-chain problem}, where the sparse reward at the end of the chain is replaced with $R(\schain{D},\aR)=\frac{1}{2}$. In this case the optimal policy takes the immediate reward of $R(\schain{1},\aL)=\frac{D-1}{D}$ from the initial state, and as illustrated in Figure \ref{fig:d_chain_results}, the maximum entropy objective is misaligned with the standard objective for a temperature $\alpha > \frac{1}{5}$.

        \includegraphics[width=0.49\textwidth]{figures/ch4/ten_chain.png}
        \includegraphics[width=0.49\textwidth]{figures/ch4/mod_ten_chain.png}
        \caption[Results on the (modified) D-chain problem.]{Results on the D-chain problem (left), and the modified D-chain problem (right), for a variety of parameters and with $D=10$. Each algorithm was run for 5000 trials, and results are averaged over 25 repeated experiments. For BTS the temperature function is set to a constant value, and in DENTS the temperature function and entropy weighting function are set to the same constant value. \todo{update figures to have y-axis 0 to 1}}

    \end{figure}

\subsection{The Entropy Trap}
\label{sec:4-3-2-entropy_trap}

    \begin{figure}
        \centering
        \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figures/ch4/entropy_trap_mdp_colour.pdf}
        \caption[An illustration of the \textit{Entropy Trap problem}.]{An illustration of the \textit{Entropy Trap problem}. In red, the original D-chain problem (Figure \ref{fig:modified_d_chain}) is labelled as the UCT Gauntlet, and the additional states added for the Entropy Trap are in blue. If a reward is not specified for a transition it is zero. \todo{rephrase labels to D-Chain and Entropy Chain}}
        \label{fig:entropy_trap}
    \end{figure}

    In the \textit{D-chain with Entropy Trap problem} (or \textit{Entropy Trap problem} for short), an additional chain of states is appended to the end of the D-chain, designed to exploit the misalignment between the maximum entropy and standard objectives. At the end of the D-chain, the agent must choose between collecting the sparse reward of one, or entering the \textit{entropy chain} where no reward is given but the agent can accumulate entropy ``reward''. The Entropy Trap problem is depicted in Figure \ref{fig:entropy_trap}.

    At each step of the entropy chain the agent still has the option of the two actions $\aL$ and $\aR$. The optimal maximum entropy policy is uniform, taking either action with probability $\frac{1}{2}$ with an entropy of $\log(2)$ per step. If there are $E$ steps in the entropy chain and a temperature of $\alpha$ is used in the maximum entropy objective, then the maximum entropy bonus that can be collected from the chain is $\alpha E\log(2)$. Therefore, $\frac{1}{E\log(2)}$ is an upper bound on the misalignment threshold for the entropy trap, as if $\alpha > \frac{1}{E\log(2)}$, a maximum entropy agent will select the entropy chain over the reward of one at the end of the D-chain. 

    The entropy trap problem can therefore be made arbitrarily difficult for UCT by increasing $D$, while the misalignment threshold can be made arbitrarily small by increasing $E$. Theorem \ref{thrm:ments_bad_mdp} uses the case when $D=E$ to show that MENTS must either suffer from misalignment or require an exponential number of trials with respect to the size of the state space.
    %
    \begin{restatable}{theorem}{thrmMENTSbadmdp} \label{thrm:ments_bad_mdp}
        For any value of $\alphaments$, either the maximum entropy objective is misaligned, or MENTS requires an exponential number of trials to recommend the optimal policy.

        More precisely, after $n$ trials of MENTS: $\Pr(\psiments\neq \pi^*) > \frac{1}{2}$ if $n\leq 2^{D-1}$; and either $\mathbb{E}\left[\sreg(s_0,\psiments)\right]\not\rightarrow 0$ or there exist constants $c,k>0$ such that $\mathbb{E}\left[\sreg(s_0,\psiments)\right] \geq c\left(1 - \frac{n}{k^{|\cl{S}|}}\right)$.
    \end{restatable}

    Results on the Entropy Trap problem are given in Figure \ref{fig:entropy_trap_results} for $(D,E)=(10,10)$ and $(D,E)=(15,25)$. In the $(D,E)=(15,25)$ case, $500{,}000$ trials were run per parameter setting. The misalignment threshold is bounded above by $\frac{1}{25\log(2)}\approx 0.06$, which is prohibitively small for effective exploration. Below this threshold, no more than 1000 of the $500{,}000$ trials reached the third state of the D-chain, and typically fewer than 100. While BTS and MaxUCT are able to produce the optimal policy given a sufficiently large temperature/bias parameter, increasing $D$ further would eventually prevent them from doing so within a reasonable number of trials. DENTS, by contrast, is able to recommend the optimal policy across a wider range of settings since its Bellman value estimates are unaffected by the entropy chain. For BTS and DENTS, each of $\alphabts$, $\alphadents$ and $\betadents$ is set to a constant value. While this leads to the majority of trials in DENTS entering the entropy chain, this can be resolved by using a function that tends to zero for $\betadents$.

        \includegraphics[width=0.49\textwidth]{figures/ch4/entropy_trap_15.png}
        \caption[Results on the Entropy Trap problem.]{Results on the Entropy Trap problem, with $(D,E)=(10,10)$ (left) and $(D,E)=(15,25)$ (right). Each algorithm was run with a variety of temperature and bias parameters, averaged over 25 repeated experiments.     \end{figure}

\subsection{Environments}
\label{sec:4-4-1-envs}

    Gridworld environments are used to investigate the 
    behaviour of the search algorithms in controlled, solvable 
    settings. Three gridworld environments are used: a 
    heavily stochastic environment with sparse rewards 
    (FrozenLake), a deterministic environment with sparse 
    rewards (DeterministicGridWorld), and a mildly stochastic environment 
    with dense rewards (the Sailing Problem).
    


\subsubsection{FrozenLake}
\label{sec:4-4-1-1-frozenlake}
    \textit{FrozenLake} \cite{gymnasium} is a grid world environment where the agent aims to reach a goal state while avoiding holes that it could fall into. The agent can attempt to move in any cardinal direction at each time step. At each step, there is an equal probability that the agent moves in the intended direction or slips and moves in a perpendicular direction. For example, if the agent tries to move down, then there is a probability of $\frac{1}{3}$ that the agent moves down, moves left or moves right. If the agent moves or slips into a wall, then the agent remains in the same location. 
    
    If the agent reaches the goal state, then a reward of one is received. If the agent falls into a hole, then the trial ends and zero reward is received. Consequently, the value of a policy $\pi$ at a state $s$ is the probability that the goal is reached under $\pi$.

    Figure \ref{fig:frozen_lake_pic} illustrates the environment. 
    A range of maps of different sizes is used in the experiments, from 4x4 to 4x16, which are specified in Appendix \ref{appsec:4:envs}.

\subsubsection{DeterministicGridWorld}
\label{sec:4-4-1-2-dgw}
    \textit{DeterministicGridWorld} (DGW) is used to refer to the deterministic version of FrozenLake in these experiments. The environment is the same as FrozenLake, but the agent cannot slip and move in a perpendicular direction. To differentiate between paths of different lengths a discount factor of $\gamma$ is used, so that shorter paths gain more reward than longer ones. That is, if the agent reaches a goal after $t$ timesteps, then a reward of $\gamma^t$ is received. Maps of sizes 8x8 to 8x32, defined in Appendix \ref{appsec:4:envs}, are used in the experiments for this environment.

\subsubsection{Go}
\label{sec:4-4-1-4-go}

    For a more challenging domain, a round-robin tournament was run on the game of Go, which has widely motivated the development of MCTS methods \cite{offlineonlineuct,alpha_go,alpha_go_zero}. \textit{Area scoring} is used to score the games, with a \textit{komi} (a score handicap for black) of $7.5$. 

    The methods described in this chapter can be adapted for two-player games by using a discount factor in the MDP. The value of a policy $\pi$ with a discount factor $\gamma$ is defined as:
    \begin{align}
        V^{\pi,\gamma}(s;t) = \bb{E}_{\tau\sim\pi}\left[\sum_{i=t}^{H-1} \gamma^{i-t}r_i \Bigg| s_t=s \right].
    \end{align}
    %
    If the two players are using the policies $\pi^{(0)}$ and $\pi^{(1)}$, and $\pi$ is the policy that interleaves $\pi^{(0)}$ at even timesteps with $\pi^{(1)}$ at odd timesteps, then using a discount factor of $\gamma=-1$ is sufficient to handle two-player games where a reward of $+1$ is given when a player wins\footnote{The score of $-1$ for losing a game is accounted for by the discount factor $\gamma=-1$.}.

\subsection{Evaluation Procedure} 
\label{sec:4-4-2-eval_procedure}


\subsubsection{Gridworld Environments}
    Consider an algorithm with search tree $\cl{T}$, which provides a partial recommendation policy $\psi_{\alg}$. The recommendation policy is made complete by using a uniformly random policy for states and actions that are outside of the tree $\cl{T}$ as follows:
    % 
    \begin{align}
        \psi(a|s) =
        \begin{cases}
            \psi_{\alg}(a|s)  & \text{ if } s\in\cl{T}, \\
            \frac{1}{|\cl{A}|}      & \text{ otherwise.}
        \end{cases} \label{eq:full_Recommend}
    \end{align}
    
    Using the completed policy $\psi$, a Monte Carlo estimate of $V^{\psi}(s_0)$ is computed by sampling a number of trajectories from $\psi$ and averaging the returns. Unless otherwise stated, each Monte Carlo estimate consists of 1024 trials, and results are averaged over 25 repeated experiments.

    The BayesOpt package \cite{bayesopt} was used to tune hyperparameters for the algorithms in the gridworld environments. Appendix \ref{appsec:4:hps} gives details on the hyperparameters used and how they were tuned.



\subsubsection{Go}
    In the Go experiments, each algorithm was given $5$ seconds of compute time per move and allowed to use $32$ search threads and access to a single Nvidia V100 GPU on a shared compute cluster. Additionally, each algorithm has access to a value network $\tilde{V}$ and prior policy network $\tilde{\pi}$ provided by the open-source library KataGo \cite{katago}. $\tilde{V}$ is used for the value initialisation function $\Vinit$, and $\tilde{\pi}$ is mixed into the search policy as described in Section \ref{sec:4-2-3-stoch_search_policies}.

    For all algorithms, the \textit{most visited} recommendation 
    policy is used to recommend actions (e.g. Equation 
    \todo{ref dents eqn}), as it is more robust than using 
    value estimates directly: a largely unvisited action may 
    receive a noisy neural network evaluation shortly before 
    the recommendation is made, which the most visited policy 
    avoids by using the visit statistics instead.

    Hyperparameters were tuned using round-robin tournaments on 9x9 Go boards, details are given in Appendix \ref{appsec:round_robin}.

    The PUCT algorithm is used as a baseline, as described in Alpha Go Zero \cite{alpha_go_zero}. For maximum entropy baselines, AR-MENTS, AR-RENTS and AR-TENTS (Appendix \ref{appsec:go_ar_ments}) are used. These are variants of MENTS, RENTS and TENTS using average return backups. It is experimentally shown in Appendix \ref{appsec:go_ar_vs_bellman} that the average returns algorithms decisively beat the Bellman versions. The Alias method (Section \ref{sec:4-2-3-stoch_search_policies}) is used for all algorithms using stochastic search policies.

    distributing it more uniformly as softmax does. In combination

    resembling breadth-first search, leading to more thorough 
    exploration on larger maps but slower initial progress, as visible in the 8x8 map.

    This experiment investigates how the temperature parameter 
    affects algorithm performance in practice, and whether 
    the entropy misalignment problem identified in Section 
    \ref{sec:4-1-intro} manifests empirically in gridworld 
    environments.

    Figures \ref{fig:4:dgw_param_sens} and \ref{fig:4:fl_param_sens} show results where each algorithm is run for $100{,}000$ trials for a range of parameter settings. Values are estimated with 256 Monte Carlo trials and results are averaged over 10 repeated experiments.

    For BTS and DENTS, the temperature and entropy coefficient 
    functions are set to constant values, 
    \begin{align}
        \alphadents(x) = \betadents(x) = \alpha_{\text{const}},
    \end{align} 
    with $\alpha_{\text{const}}$ varied across the range shown.
    
    As shown in Figures \ref{fig:4:dgw_param_sens} and 
    \ref{fig:4:fl_param_sens}, when the temperature 
    parameter of MENTS or TENTS is sufficiently large, the 
    value of the recommended policy falls to zero, 
    consistent with the misalignment behaviour predicted 
    theoretically.

    Moreover, in Figure \ref{fig:4:fl_param_sens} it can be seen that the most effective temperature for exploration is around the misalignment threshold. This is consistent with Figure \ref{fig:4:fl_results} where MENTS suffered from misalignment on the larger maps. The latter is explained by the larger maps containing more open spaces without holes, providing greater opportunity to 
    gather entropy rewards and effectively lowering the 
    misalignment threshold.

    Empirically, RENTS does not exhibit entropy 
    misalignment. This can be understood by again noting that 
    the relative entropy objective encourages 
    the search policy to repeat the same action across 
    consecutive timesteps within a trial, which in 
    the 8x8 DGW and 4x4 FrozenLake maps is an effective strategy.
    For example, in the 8x8 DGW map (Figure \ref{appfig:fl_maps}) there is a path around the edge of the map from the starting state to the goal, where the optimal path involves sustained movement in a single direction.

    RENTS also performs well at low temperature, where the search policy is nearly greedy, even when $\epsilon_{\text{RENTS}}=0$. 
    The RENTS regularisation 
    will still bias early trials towards sustained sequences of 
    the same action.

    \begin{figure}
        \centering
        \includegraphics[width=0.6\textwidth]{figures/ch4/plots/cr_701_fl_8x8_param_e0_dt1.png}
        \caption[DeterministicGridWorld results with decaying 
        temperatures.]{A repetition of Figure 
        \ref{fig:4:dgw_param_sens:sub:a} using decaying 
        temperatures for MENTS, RENTS and TENTS. For all 
        algorithms $\alg$, the temperature function 
        $\alpha_{\alg}(x) = \frac{\alpha_{\text{init}}}{\sqrt{1+x/10}}$ 
        is used. }
        \label{fig:4:dgw_param_sens_decay}
    \end{figure}

    A natural question is whether replacing the fixed 
    temperature in MENTS and TENTS with a decaying 
    schedule, analogous to that used in BTS and DENTS, 
    would resolve the misalignment problem. However, 
    recall the maximum entropy objective 
    (Section~\ref{sec:2-3-1-merl}): 
    \begin{align}
        J_{\sft}(\pi) = \bb{E}_{\tau\sim\pi}\left[\sum_{i=t}^{H-1} r_i + \alpha\cl{H}(\pi(\cdot|s_i)) \right]. \label{eq:4:merl_obj_fn}
    \end{align}

    Using a decaying temperature in MENTS and TENTS will 
    in effect give each term in the entropy sum of Equation (\ref{eq:4:merl_obj_fn}) its own coefficient, so the objective is still misaligned because of large temperatures deeper in the tree. Experimentally this is confirmed to persist in Figure \ref{fig:4:dgw_param_sens_decay}, where the temperature is set to the function 
    \begin{align}
        \alpha_{\alg}(x) = \frac{\alpha_{\text{init}}}{\sqrt{1+x/10}},
    \end{align}
    for $\alg\in\{\ments, \text{RENTS}, \text{TENTS}\}$.

    DENTS overcomes this issue via its entropy values which estimate the entire entropy sum of Equation (\ref{eq:4:merl_obj_fn}), which can be decayed independently at each node by reducing $\betadents(x)$.

\subsection{Notation}
\label{sec:4-5-1-notation}

    % 0. Clarifying notation 
    Because of the unique state assumption (Section \ref{sec:2-4-1-notation}), any state $s_t \in \tau = (s_0,a_0,r_0,\ldots,s_{H-1},a_{H-1},r_{H-1},s_{H})$ may only appear at timestep $t$ in any trajectory. Throughout this chapter, $s_t$ and $a_t$ are used to denote an arbitrary state and action at an arbitrary timestep $t$.

    When making arguments that apply to multiple algorithms, the general policies $\pi$ and $\psi$ are used. When making arguments about specific algorithms, subscripts are used, e.g. $\pibts$. 

    % 1. Introduce trial superscripts
    To reason about MCTS processes over multiple trials, the existing notation is extended with superscripts to track both the number of trials run and the number of times each node has been visited.

    Most variables are denoted with a superscript $n$ to denote that it is the value used on the $n$th trial of MCTS. For example:
    \begin{itemize}
        \item 
            If an algorithm uses a search policy $\pi$ and outputs a recommendation policy $\psi$, then the search policy followed on the $n$th trial is denoted $\pi^n$, and the recommendation policy after $n$ trials is denoted $\psi^n$. 
        \item 
            When it is necessary to reason about multiple trajectories, the superscript notation will also be used. The trajectory sampled in the $n$th trial is $\tau^n=(s_0^n,a_0^n,r_0^n,\ldots,s_{H-1}^n,a_{H-1}^n,r_{H-1}^n,s_{H}^n)$. If it is sampled from a search policy $\pi^n$, then $\tau^n\sim\pi^n$.
        \item 
            The search tree after $n$ trials is denoted $\cl{T}^n$, the initial search tree is $\cl{T}^0=\{s_0\}$, and $\cl{T}^n = \cl{T}^{n-1} \cup \{s\in\tau^n\} \cup \{(s,a)\in\tau^n\}$.
    \end{itemize}

    % 2. Introduce visit index sets + define visit counts
    Let $T^n(s_t)$ and $T^n(s_t,a_t)$ denote the sets of trial indices on which $s_t$ was visited and action $a_t$ was selected respectively, in the first $n$ trials, where $t$ is the timestep at which the node appears in the trajectory:
    %
    \begin{align}
        T^n(s_t) &= \{i | 1\leq i\leq n, s^i_t = s_t \} \label{eq:4:index_set_s} \\
        T^n(s_t,a_t) &= \{i | 1\leq i\leq n, s^i_t = s_t, a^i_t = a_t \} \label{eq:4:index_set_sa}.
    \end{align}

    The visit counts after $n$ trials are defined as follows, with $s_t\in\cl{S}$ and $a_t\in\cl{A}$:
    %
    \begin{align}
        N^n(s_t) &= \sum_{i=1}^n \one[s^i_t = s_t] = |T^n(s_t)|, \\
        N^n(s_t,a_t) &= \sum_{i=1}^n \one[s^i_t = s_t \cap a^i_t = a_t] = |T^n(s_t,a_t)|.
    \end{align}

    % 3. Connect index sets to visit counts
    Under the unique state assumption, the visit counts can also be expressed in the following equivalent forms, which are more convenient for the proofs:
    %
    \begin{align}
    N^n(s_t,a_t) &= \sum_{i\in T^n(s_t)} \one[a^i_t = a_t], 
        \label{eq:4:nsa_sum} \\
    N^n(s_{t+1}) &= \sum_{i\in T^n(s_t,a_t)} 
        \one[s^i_{t+1} = s_{t+1}] = |T^n(s_t,a_t)|. 
        \label{eq:4:ns_sum}
    \end{align}
    %
    The key observation is that $N^n(s_t,a_t)$ can be computed as a sum over the $N^n(s_t)$ trials on which $s_t$ was visited, and $N^n(s_{t+1})$ as a sum over the $N^n(s_t,a_t)$ trials on which $a_t$ was selected from $s_t$.

    % 4. Introduce backup superscripts + give backup equations as an example
    Value estimates are the one exception to the superscript notation to denote the number of trials. Superscripts for value estimates denote the number of times that the value has been backed up. To differentiate this counting with respect to backups, instead of with respect to trials, a superscript with brackets will be used. For example, the backups for DENTS value estimates would be written as follows. Let $\tau^n \sim \mpidents{n}$ be a trajectory sampled from the DENTS search policy on the $n$th trial. Let $\tau^n=(s_0,a_0,r_0,\ldots,s_{h-1},a_{h-1},r_{h-1},s_{h})$. The backups for $t=h-1,\ldots,0$ are then: 
    %
    \begin{align}
        \mQdents{N^n(s_t,a_t)}(s_t,a_t) &= 
            R(s_t,a_t) + \sum_{s' \in \suc{s_t}{a_t}} \left( 
                \frac{N^n(s')}{N^n(s_t,a_t)} \mVdents{N^n(s')}(s') \right), 
            \label{appeq:dp_q_backup} \\ 
        \mVdents{N^n(s_t)}(s_t) &=\max_{a\in\cl{A}} \mQdents{N^n(s_t,a)}(s_t,a), 
            \label{appeq:dp_v_backup} \\
        \mHQdents{N^n(s_t,a_t)}(s_t,a_t) &= 
            \sum_{s'\in \suc{s_t}{a_t}} \frac{N^n(s')}{N^n(s_t,a_t)} \mHVdents{N^n(s')}(s'), \\
        \mHVdents{N^n(s_t)}(s_t) &= 
            \cl{H}(\mpidents{n}(\cdot | s_t)) 
                + \sum_{a\in\cl{A}} \mpidents{n}(a_t|s_t)\mHQdents{N^n(s_t,a_t)}(s_t,a_t).
    \end{align}

\subsection{Preliminaries}
\label{sec:4-5-2-prelim}

    This subsection introduces definitions and preliminary lemmas that are used in the proofs of Sections \ref{sec:4-5-3-consistency} and \ref{sec:4-5-4-entropy-trap}.






    % ----------------------------
    % Immediate Regret <-> Simple Regret
    % ----------------------------

    The proofs in this section make use of the \textit{immediate simple regret}, which measures the regret incurred by taking a single action step rather than following the full policy. Working with immediate simple regret simplifies the proofs, since bounds and convergence results transfer between the two notions via Lemma \ref{lem:imm_regr_to_simp_regr}.
    %
    \begin{defn}
        \label{def:4:immediate_simple_regret}
        The \textnormal{immediate simple regret} is defined as:
        %
        \begin{align}
            \immreg(s_t,\psi^n) =& V^*(s_t) - \bb{E}_{a_t\sim\psi^n(s_t)}[Q^*(s_t,a_t)].
        \end{align}
    \end{defn}



    
    The following lemma shows that asymptotic bounds and convergence results transfer between immediate and simple regret, so it suffices to prove results about immediate simple regret.

    \begin{lemma} 
        \label{lem:imm_regr_to_simp_regr}
        $\bb{E}\left[\sreg(s_t,\psi^n)\right]=O(f(n))$ for all $s_t\in\cl{S}_0$ if and only if $\bb{E}\left[\immreg(s_t,\psi^n)\right]=O(f(n))$ for all $s_t\in\cl{S}_0$. Consequently, $\bb{E}\left[\sreg(s_t,\psi^n)\right]\rightarrow 0$ if and only if $\bb{E}\left[\immreg(s_t,\psi^n)\right]\rightarrow 0$.
    \end{lemma}
    \begin{proof}
        Firstly, the simple regret can be written recursively in terms of the immediate simple regret:
        \begin{align}
            \sreg(s_t,\psi^n)
                =& V^*(s_t) - V^{\psi^n}(s_t) \\
                =& V^*(s_t) - \bb{E}_{a_t\sim\psi^n(s_t)}[Q^{\psi^n}(s_t,a_t)] \\
                =& V^*(s_t) - \bb{E}_{a_t\sim\psi^n(s_t)}[R(s_t,a_t) +    
                    \bb{E}_{s_{t+1}\sim\Pr(\cdot|s_t,a_t)}[V^{\psi^n}(s_{t+1})]] \\
                =& V^*(s_t) - \bb{E}_{a_t\sim\psi^n(s_t)}\big[Q^*(s_t,a_t) - 
                    \bb{E}_{s_{t+1}\sim\Pr(\cdot|s_t,a_t)}[V^*(s_{t+1})]  \notag \\
                    &+ \bb{E}_{s_{t+1}\sim\Pr(\cdot|s_t,a_t)}[V^{\psi^n}(s_{t+1})]\big] \label{eq:4:local4} \\
                =& V^*(s_t) - \bb{E}_{a_t\sim\psi^n(s_t)}[Q^*(s_t,a_t)] \notag \\
                    &+ \bb{E}_{a_t\sim\psi^n(s_t),s_{t+1}\sim\Pr(\cdot|s_t,a_t)}[
                        V^*(s_{t+1}) - V^{\psi^n}(s_{t+1})] \\
                =& \immreg(s_t,\psi^n) \notag \\
                    &+ \bb{E}_{a_t\sim\psi^n(s_t),s_{t+1}\sim\Pr(\cdot|s_t,a_t)}[\sreg(s_{t+1},\psi^n)], \label{eq:4:regret_relation}
        \end{align}
        where line (\ref{eq:4:local4}) uses $R(s_t,a_t) = Q^*(s_t,a_t) - \bb{E}_{s_{t+1}\sim\Pr(\cdot|s_t,a_t)}[V^*(s_{t+1})]$, a rearrangement of the Bellman optimality equation for $Q^*(s_t,a_t)$.
        
        Immediately, this shows that $\bb{E}\left[\immreg(s_t,\psi^n)\right]\leq \bb{E}\left[\sreg(s_t,\psi^n)\right]$ and hence if $\bb{E}\left[\sreg(s_t,\psi^n)\right]=O(f(n))$ then $\bb{E}\left[\immreg(s_t,\psi^n)\right]=O(f(n))$.

        If $\bb{E}\left[\immreg(s_t,\psi^n)\right]=O(f(n))$ then by induction over $t=H,\ldots,0$, using the relation in Equation (\ref{eq:4:regret_relation}):
        \begin{align}
            \bb{E}\left[\sreg(s_t,\psi^n)\right] 
                &= \bb{E}\left[ O(f(n)) + \bb{E}_{a_t\sim\psi^n(s_t),s_{t+1}\sim\Pr(\cdot|s_t,a_t)}[O(f(n))] \right] \\
                &= O(f(n)).
        \end{align}

        The base case is $t=H$: since $s_H$ is a terminal state $\sreg(s_H,\psi^n)=\immreg(s_H,\psi^n)=0$.
    \end{proof}

    \proofsep



    
    % ----------------------------
    % DKW Inequality -> Empirical Probs Converge
    % ----------------------------
    
    The DKW inequality (Theorem \ref{thrm:2:dkw_inequality}) is used to show that the empirical transition probabilities converge in probability to the true transition probabilities.
    %
    \begin{figure}
        \centering
        \includegraphics[scale=0.6]{figures/ch4/dkw_diagram.pdf}
        \caption[Bounding the empirical transition probabilities to the true transition probabilities.]{Bounding the empirical transition probabilities to the true transition probabilities. The true cdf is shown as a solid black line, the empirical cdf is shown as a dashed green line, and a worst case error of $\varepsilon/2$, using Theorem \ref{thrm:2:dkw_inequality}, is shown in red. The probability mass of $p(s_{t+1}|s_t,a_t)$ and empirical probability mass of $\frac{N^n(s_{t+1})}{N^n(s_t,a_t)}$ is also indicated to demonstrate how the constructed distribution gives Corollary \ref{cor:4:bound_transition_distribution}.}
        \label{fig:dkw_diag}
    \end{figure}
    %
    \begin{corollary} 
        \label{cor:4:bound_transition_distribution}
        For all $(s_t,a_t)\in\cl{S}\times\cl{A}$ and for all $\varepsilon >0$:
        \begin{align}
            \Pr\left(\max_{s_{t+1}\in\suc{s_t}{a_t}}\left| \frac{N^n(s_{t+1})}{N^n(s_t,a_t)} - p(s_{t+1}|s_t,a_t) \right| > 2 \varepsilon \right) \leq 2 \exp\left(-2 \varepsilon^2 N^n(s_t,a_t) \right),
        \end{align}
        %
        as $N^n(s_t,a_t)\rightarrow\infty$. Consequently $\frac{N^n(s_{t+1})}{N^n(s_t,a_t)} \rap p(s_{t+1}|s_t,a_t)$ as $N^n(s_t,a_t)\rightarrow\infty$.
    \end{corollary}
    \begin{proofoutline}
        Consider the cumulative distribution function of the successor state distribution $p(s_{t+1}|s_t,a_t)$, using some arbitrary ordering over the successor states in $\suc{s_t}{a_t}$. The result follows by applying Theorem \ref{thrm:2:dkw_inequality} with $m=N^n(s_t,a_t)$.

        To see why the bound involves $2\varepsilon$ rather than $\varepsilon$, consider Figure \ref{fig:dkw_diag}. Applying the DKW inequality bounds the error in the cumulative distribution function by $\varepsilon$ at every point. Since the probability mass of any single successor state corresponds to the difference between two CDF values, the errors at either side may accumulate, giving a worst-case error of $2\varepsilon$ in the empirical probability of any individual successor state.
    \end{proofoutline}

\subsection{Consistency Results} 
\label{sec:4-5-3-consistency}

    This subsection proves the consistency results for \mbox{(AR-)DENTS}. The proof of Theorem \ref{thrm:4:ar_dents} is given in full, as it uses all of the preliminary lemmas. The proofs of the remaining theorems are given as outlines, highlighting only where they differ.

    Theorems \ref{thrm:4:bts}, \ref{thrm:4:bts_decay} and \ref{thrm:4:ar_bts} for \mbox{(AR-)BTS} follow directly from Theorems \ref{thrm:4:dents}, \ref{thrm:4:dents_decay} and \ref{thrm:4:ar_dents} respectively, by setting $\betadents(x)=0$ and $\betaardents(x)=0$.

    \thrmARDENTS*
    \begin{proof}
        Firstly by Corollary \ref{cor:inf_often_visit_node} (of Lemma \ref{lem:inf_often_action_select}), with the assumption that $\epsardents > 0$, all visit counts $N^n(s_t)$ and $N^n(s_t,a_t)$ will tend to infinity almost surely as $n\rightarrow\infty$.

        Then the (Q-)value estimates and search policy will converge almost surely to their optimal counterparts. This follows by induction on the timestep $t$. For the base case $t=H$ at the planning horizon, $\mVardents{N^n(s_H)}(s_H) = V^*(s_H) = 0$ by definition. The inductive steps proceed as follows, for each $s_t\in\cl{S}_0$ and $a_t\in\cl{A}$:
        \begin{enumerate}
            \item \label{itm:induction1}
                Assume that $\mVardents{N^n(s_{t+1})}(s_{t+1})\rap V^*(s_{t+1})$;
            \item \label{itm:induction2}
                By Lemma \ref{lem:q_converge}, it follows that $\mQardents{N^n(s_t,a_t)}(s_t,a_t) \rap Q^*(s_t,a_t)$ and consequently $\mpsiardents{n}(a_t|s_t)\rap\pi^*(a_t|s_t)$;
            \item \label{itm:induction3}
                Letting $\dot{Q}^n(s_t,a_t) = \mQardents{N^n(s_t,a_t)}(s_t,a_t) + \betaardents(N^n(s_t))\mHQardents{N^n(s_t,a_t)}(s_t,a_t)$, the assumption that $\betaardents(x)\rightarrow 0$ ensures that $\dot{Q}^n(s_t,a_t)\rap Q^*(s_t,a_t)$. Then using Lemma \ref{lem:q_converge_policy_converge}, with the assumption that $\alphaardents(x)\rightarrow 0$, it follows that $\mpiardents{n}(a_t|s_t)\rap \pi^*(a_t|s_t)$;
            \item \label{itm:induction4}
                Then by Lemma \ref{lem:search_policy_converge_mv_converge} it follows that $\frac{N^n(s_t,a_t)}{N^n(s_t)} \rap \pi^*(a_t|s_t)$ and consequently $\mmvardents{n}(a_t|s_t)\rap \pi^*(a_t|s_t)$;
            \item \label{itm:induction5}
                By Lemma \ref{lem:ardents_v_converge}, $\mVardents{N^n(s_t)}(s_t)\rap V^*(s_t)$.
        \end{enumerate}

        The immediate regret (Definition \ref{def:4:immediate_simple_regret}) then satisfies:
        \begin{align}
            \bb{E}\left[\immreg(s_t,\mpsiardents{n})\right] 
                &= \sum_{a\in\cl{A}} \left(V^*(s_t)-Q^*(s_t,a)\right) \mpsiardents{n}(a|s_t) \\
                &\rap \sum_{a\in\cl{A}} \left(V^*(s_t)-Q^*(s_t,a)\right) \pi^*(a|s_t) \\
                &= \bb{E}\left[\immreg(s_t,\pi^*)\right] \\
                &= 0,
        \end{align}
        %
        and similarly
        \begin{align}
            \bb{E}\left[\immreg(s_t,\mmvardents{n})\right]\rightarrow 0.
        \end{align}

        The result follows by Lemma \ref{lem:imm_regr_to_simp_regr} to lift the convergence of the immediate regret to convergence of the simple regret.
    \end{proof}

    \proofsep

    The following lemma considers a bandit setting at a single decision node, and shows that the logarithmically decaying exploration term ensures every action is selected infinitely often almost surely.

    \begin{lemma} 
        \label{lem:inf_often_action_select}
        Let $\rho^m$ be an arbitrary policy and consider the following search policy used on the $m$th round of a bandit problem:
        \begin{align} 
            \pi^m(a|s)&=(1-\lambda(m))\rho^m(a) + \lambda(m) \frac{1}{|\cl{A}|}, \label{eq:4:ioas_policy} \\
            \lambda(m) &= \min(1,\epsilon/\log(e+m)),
        \end{align}
        where $\epsilon > 0$, $s\in\cl{S}$ and $a\in\cl{A}$. Let $a^m\sim \pi^m$ be the $m$th independently sampled action, and let $M^m(a)$ be the number of times action $a$ was selected in the first $m$ rounds, i.e. $M^m(a)=\sum_{i=1}^m \one[a^i=a]$. Then $M^m(a)\raas\infty$ as $m\rightarrow \infty$ for all $a\in\cl{A}$. 
    \end{lemma}
    %
    \begin{proof} 
        Suppose that the result does not hold and there exists some $b\in\cl{A}$ such that:
        \begin{align}
            \Pr\left(M^m(b) \rightarrow \infty\right) < 1.
        \end{align}

        If $M^m(b) \not\rightarrow \infty$, then it is logically equivalent to say that there exists some round $\ell\in\bb{N}$ from which $b$ is never sampled again (with $\ell \geq \exp(\epsilon+1)$). That is:
        \begin{align}
            \Pr\left(\bigcap_{m=\ell}^\infty a^m \neq b\right)
                = \Pr\left(M^m(b) \not\rightarrow \infty\right) 
                > 0. \label{eq:4:ioas_contradict}
        \end{align}

        However, from Equation (\ref{eq:4:ioas_policy}) action $b$ is sampled with probability at least:
        \begin{align}
            \Pr\left(a^m = b\right) \geq \frac{\lambda(m)}{|\cl{A}|} = \frac{\epsilon}{|\cl{A}| \log(e+m)}.
        \end{align} 

        So the probability of never sampling action $b$ again after the first $\ell-1$ rounds is at most:
        \begin{align}
            \Pr\left(\bigcap_{m=\ell}^\infty a^m \neq b \right)

                    \Pr(a^m \neq b) \\
                &\leq \lim_{k\rightarrow\infty} \prod_{m=\ell}^k 
                    \left( 1 - \frac{\epsilon}{|\cl{A}|\log(e+k)} \right) \\
                &\leq \lim_{k\rightarrow\infty} 
                    \left( 1 - \frac{\epsilon}{|\cl{A}|\log(e+k)} \right)^{k-\ell} \label{eq:4:ioas_one} \\
                &= 0, \label{eq:4:ioas_two}

    As a consequence of Lemma \ref{lem:inf_often_action_select}, every reachable state (and state-action pair) will be visited infinitely often in any MCTS process using a search policy of the form given in Equation (\ref{eq:4:ioas_policy}).

    The following corollary lifts Lemma \ref{lem:inf_often_action_select} to the full tree search setting, where the search policy is indexed by the number of trials $n$, but the lemma is applied at each node with respect to its visit count $m=N^n(s_t)$.

    \begin{corollary} 
        \label{cor:inf_often_visit_node}
        For all $s_t\in\cl{S}_0$ and $a_t\in\cl{A}$, any MCTS process using a search policy of the following form on the $n$th trial:
        \begin{align}
            \pi^n(a_t|s_t)&=(1-\lambda(s_t,\epsilon))\rho^n(a_t|s_t) + \lambda(s_t,\epsilon) \frac{1}{|\cl{A}|}, \\
            \lambda(s_t,\epsilon) &= \min(1,\epsilon/\log(e+N^n(s_t))),
        \end{align}
        %
        where $\rho^n$ is an arbitrary policy and $\epsilon > 0$. All reachable states and state-action pairs will be visited infinitely often. That is $N^n(s_t)\raas\infty$ and $N^n(s_t,a_t)\raas\infty$ as $n\rightarrow\infty$.
    \end{corollary}
    \begin{proofoutline}
        The result follows by induction over Lemma \ref{lem:inf_often_action_select}. At $t=0$ the result holds directly from the lemma with $m=n$. For the inductive step, if $N^n(s_t,a_t)\raas\infty$ then $N^n(s_{t+1})\raas\infty$ since $s_{t+1}$ is sampled from $p(\cdot|s_t,a_t)$ with positive probability, then $N^n(s_{t+1},a_{t+1})\raas\infty$ by applying Lemma \ref{lem:inf_often_action_select} with $m=N^n(s_{t+1})$.
    \end{proofoutline}

    \proofsep

    % V converge => Q converge 
    % ----------------------------
    The following lemma shows that if the value estimates converge to the optimal values, then the Q-value estimates computed via a sample-weighted average of child node values also converge to the optimal Q-values. 

    \begin{lemma}
        \label{lem:q_converge}
        For all $s_t\in\cl{S}_0$, $a_t\in\cl{A}$, if $N^n(s_t,a_t)\rightarrow\infty$ and $\dot{V}^{(N^n(s'))}(s')\rap V^*(s')$ for all $s'\in\suc{s_t}{a_t}$, then the Q-values computed by:
        \begin{align}
            \dot{Q}^{(N^n(s_t,a_t))}(s_t,a_t) = R(s_t,a_t) + \sum_{s' \in \suc{s_t}{a_t}} 
                \frac{N^n(s')}{N^n(s_t,a_t)} \dot{V}^{(N^n(s'))}(s'),
        \end{align}
        converge to the optimal Q-values: $\dot{Q}^{(N^n(s_t,a_t))}(s_t,a_t) \rap Q^*(s_t,a_t)$.
    \end{lemma}
    \begin{proofoutline}
        By Corollary \ref{cor:4:bound_transition_distribution}, 
        $\frac{N^n(s')}{N^n(s_t,a_t)} \rap p(s'|s_t,a_t)$ as $N^n(s_t,a_t)\rightarrow\infty$.
        %
        Then:
        \begin{align}
            \dot{Q}^{(N^n(s_t,a_t))}(s_t,a_t) 
                &= R(s_t,a_t) + \sum_{s' \in \suc{s_t}{a_t}} 
                    \frac{N^n(s')}{N^n(s_t,a_t)} \dot{V}^{(N^n(s'))}(s') \\
                &\rap R(s_t,a_t) + \sum_{s' \in \suc{s_t}{a_t}} 
                    p(s'|s_t,a_t) V^*(s') \\
                &= Q^*(s_t,a_t).
        \end{align}
    \end{proofoutline}

    \proofsep

    % Q val converge => Policy converge
    % ----------------------------

    For Boltzmann search policies, if the Q-value estimates converge to the optimal Q-values and the temperature tends to zero, then the search policy converges to the optimal policy.

    \begin{lemma}
        \label{lem:q_converge_policy_converge}
        If a search policy of the following form is used in MCTS:
        \begin{align}
            \pi^n(a_t|s_t)&=(1-\lambda(s_t,\epsilon))\rho^n(a_t|s_t) + \lambda(s_t,\epsilon) \frac{1}{|\cl{A}|}, \\
            \rho^n &\propto \exp\left(\frac{\dot{Q}^n(s_t,a_t)}{\alpha(N^n(s_t))}\right), \\
            \lambda(s_t,\epsilon) &= \min(1,\epsilon/\log(e+N^n(s_t))),
        \end{align}
        %
        where $\epsilon > 0$, $\alpha(x)\rightarrow 0$ and $\dot{Q}^n(s_t,a_t) \rap Q^*(s_t,a_t)$ as $n\rightarrow\infty$ for all $s_t\in\cl{S}_0$ and $a_t\in\cl{A}$. Then $\pi^n(a_t|s_t) \rap \pi^*(a_t|s_t)$ as $n\rightarrow\infty$.
    \end{lemma}
    %
    \begin{proofoutline}
        From Corollary \ref{cor:inf_often_visit_node}, $N^n(s_t)\raas\infty$ as $n\rightarrow\infty$, and hence $\lambda(s_t,\epsilon)\raas 0$, so $\pi^n(a_t|s_t) - \rho^n(a_t|s_t) \raas 0$.

        It is known that a Boltzmann distribution converges to the one-hot distribution over the argmax as the temperature tends to zero: if $\alpha(N^n(s_t))\rightarrow 0$ and $\dot{Q}^n(s_t,a_t)\rap Q^*(s_t,a_t)$, then $\rho^n(a_t|s_t) \rap \one[a_t = \argmax_{a\in\cl{A}} Q^*(s_t,a)] = \pi^*(a_t|s_t)$.

        Combining these two results gives $\pi^n(a_t|s_t)\rap\pi^*(a_t|s_t)$.
    \end{proofoutline}

    \proofsep




    % ----------------------------
    % Search Policy converge => Most Visited Recommendation Policy converge
    % ----------------------------

    The following lemma shows that if the search policy converges to a limiting policy under which one action is strictly preferred at every state, then the most-visited recommendation policy converges to that uniquely preferred action.

    \begin{lemma}
        \label{lem:search_policy_converge_mv_converge}
        Suppose $\pi^n \raas \pi_{\lim}$ as $n\rightarrow\infty$, where $\pi_{\lim}$ is a policy maximised by some action $a^*_t$. That is, $\pi_{\lim}(a^*_t|s_t) > \pi_{\lim}(a_t|s_t)$ for all $a_t \in \cl{A} - \{a^*_t\}$ and $s_t\in\cl{S}_0$. Then the empirical action frequencies converge almost surely to the limiting policy:
        \begin{align}
            \frac{N^n(s_t,a_t)}{N^n(s_t)} \raas \pi_{\lim}(a_t|s_t).
        \end{align}
        %
        Consequently, since $\pi_{\lim}$ has a unique maximising action $a^*_t = \argmax_{a\in\cl{A}} \pi_{\lim}(a|s_t)$ at every state, the most-visited recommendation policy converges to always recommending $a^*_t$.
    \end{lemma}
    \begin{proof}
        \begin{align}
            \frac{N^n(s_t,a_t)}{N^n(s_t)}
                &=  \sum_{i\in T^n(s_t)} \frac{\one[a_t^i = a_t]}{N^n(s_t)} \label{eq:4:local5}\\
                &\raas \lim_{n\rightarrow\infty} \sum_{i\in T^n(s_t)} \frac{\pi^i(a_t|s_t)}{N^n(s_t)} \\
                &= \pi_{\lim}(a_t|s_t),
        \end{align}
        where line (\ref{eq:4:local5}) uses Equation (\ref{eq:4:nsa_sum}). The almost sure convergence follows from Kolmogorov's criterion\footnote{A form of the Strong Law of Large Numbers for independent but \textit{not} necessarily identically distributed random variables.} \cite{intro_to_prob_theory}. The final equality uses Corollary \ref{cor:inf_often_visit_node} and Stolz-Ces\`{a}ro's theorem: since $\pi^n(a_t|s_t)\raas\pi_{\lim}(a_t|s_t)$ by assumption, the running average converges to $\pi_{\lim}(a_t|s_t)$.
        
        Since $\frac{N^n(s_t,a_t)}{N^n(s_t)} \raas \pi_{\lim}(a_t|s_t)$ and $\pi_{\lim}$ has a unique maximising action $a^*_t$ at every state, it follows that $\argmax_{a\in\cl{A}} N^n(s_t,a) \raas a^*_t$, so the most-visited recommendation policy (e.g. Equation (\ref{eq:4:mvdents})) converges to always recommending $a^*_t$.
    \end{proof}

    \proofsep




    % ----------------------------
    % Search Policy converge + Q converge => V converge
    % ----------------------------
    The next two lemmas show that the state value estimates converge to the optimal values, for DENTS and AR-DENTS respectively, given that the Q-value estimates have already converged.

    For DENTS, the state value is computed as the maximum over Q-values, so convergence follows directly.

    \begin{lemma}
        \label{lem:dents_v_converge}
        If $N^n(s_t)\raas\infty$ and $\mQdents{N^n(s_t,a_t)}(s_t,a_t)\rap Q^*(s_t,a_t)$ for all $s_t\in\cl{S}_0$ and $a_t\in\cl{A}$, then $\mVdents{N^n(s_t)}(s_t)\rap V^*(s_t)$.
    \end{lemma}
    \begin{proofoutline}
        From the definition of the DENTS state value backup (Equation (\ref{appeq:dp_v_backup})), it follows that:
        \begin{align}
            \mVdents{N^n(s_t)}(s_t) 
                &=\max_{a\in\cl{A}} \mQdents{N^n(s_t,a)}(s_t,a) \\
                &\rap \max_{a\in\cl{A}} Q^*(s_t,a) \\
                &= V^*(s_t).
        \end{align}
    \end{proofoutline}

    \proofsep



    For AR-DENTS, the state value is computed as a visit-count weighted average of Q-values, so convergence additionally requires the search policy to converge.

    \begin{lemma}
        \label{lem:ardents_v_converge}
        If $N^n(s_t)\raas\infty$, $\piardents^n(a_t|s_t)\rap\pi^*(a_t|s_t)$ and $\mQardents{N^n(s_t,a_t)}(s_t,a_t)\rap Q^*(s_t,a_t)$ for all $s_t\in\cl{S}_0$ and $a_t\in\cl{A}$, then $\mVardents{N^n(s_t)}(s_t)\rap V^*(s_t)$.
    \end{lemma}
    \begin{proofoutline}
        An equivalent equation, computed using child Q-values and visit counts, for the AR-DENTS state value backup is:
        \begin{align}
            \mVardents{N^n(s_t)}(s_t) 
                &=\sum_{a\in\cl{A}} \frac{N^n(s_t,a)}{N^n(s_t)} \mQardents{N^n(s_t,a)}(s_t,a).
        \end{align}

        By Lemma \ref{lem:search_policy_converge_mv_converge}, 
        $\frac{N^n(s_t,a)}{N^n(s_t)} \rap \pi^*(a|s_t)$ as $N^n(s_t)\rightarrow\infty$. It follows that:
        \begin{align}
            \mVardents{N^n(s_t)}(s_t) 
                &\rap \sum_{a\in\cl{A}} \pi^*(a|s_t) Q^*(s_t,a) \\
                &= V^*(s_t).
        \end{align}
    \end{proofoutline}

    \proofsep

\subsection{The Entropy Trap}
\label{sec:4-5-4-entropy-trap}

    This subsection provides the proof of Theorem \ref{thrm:ments_bad_mdp}, that informally says that for any temperature either the maximum entropy objective is misaligned, or MENTS requires an exponential number of trials to recommend the optimal policy. The core of the argument reduces to bounding the \textit{hitting time} of the state $\schain{E}$ --- the number of trials required for MENTS to first reach the end of the D-chain portion of the Entropy Trap MDP. For reference, the Entropy Trap MDP is repeated in Figure \ref{fig:entropy_trap_repeat}, with additional state labels added for the proof.


    \begin{figure}
        \centering
        \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figures/ch4/entropy_trap_mdp_colour.pdf}
        \caption[(Repeated). An illustration of the \textit{Entropy Trap problem}.]{(Repeated). An illustration of the \textit{Entropy Trap problem}, with state labels added for reference in the proof below. \todo{Update figure to include an additional state s to the E and s to the F}}
        \label{fig:entropy_trap_repeat}
    \end{figure}

    



    % \newcommand{\aL}{a^L_{\cl{M}}}
    % \newcommand{\aR}{a^R_{\cl{M}}}
    % \newcommand{\schain}[1]{s^{#1}_{\cl{M}}}
    
    \thrmMENTSbadmdp*
    
    \begin{proofoutline}
        Proof is by construction. Consider the Entropy Trap MDP defined in Figure \ref{fig:entropy_trap_repeat}, which is parameterised by $D$ the length of the D-chain, and $K$, half the number of states in the entropy trap. In this proof, the case where $K=D$ is considered.

        Note that for clarity, the full superscript notation established in Section \ref{sec:4-5-1-notation} is dropped in this proof outline to avoid confusion with the Entropy Trap state notation $\schain{i}$.

        Two cases need to be considered: when the temperature is sufficiently high for the maximum entropy objective to be misaligned; and, when the temperature is lower than this threshold.
        
        \paragraph{Case 1: $\alphaments>\frac{1}{\log(2)D}$ (Maximum entropy objective is misaligned)} $ $ \\
        
        When $\alphaments>\frac{1}{\log(2)D}$, the optimal soft value of the state $\schain{F}$ is greater than one, and so action $\aL$ is preferred over $\aR$ at state $\schain{E}$. Let $\phi$ be a uniform policy. Then:
        \begin{align}
            Q_{\sft}^*(\schain{E}, \aL) 
                &= V_{\sft}^*(\schain{F}) \\
                &= 0 + \alphaments \cdot \mathcal{H}(\phi) \\
                &= \alphaments \cdot \log(2) D \\
                & > 1 \\
                &= Q_{\sft}^*(\schain{E}, \aR).
        \end{align}

        Hence the maximum entropy objective is misaligned: $\mathbb{E}\left[\sreg(\schain{1},\psiments)\right] \rightarrow 1 > 0$.

        \paragraph{Case 2: $\alphaments\leq\frac{1}{\log(2)D}$} $ $ \\

        In this case, the temperature is sufficiently low that the MENTS search policy is nearly greedy. Hence MENTS will have a small probability of ever having a trial hitting state $\schain{E}$. For now, assume that $\Vments(\schain{E}) = 0$, and $N(\schain{E}) = 0$.
        
        First consider the composed and simplified soft backup on the D-chain states for $1\leq i\leq D$:
        \begin{align}
            \Vments(\schain{i}) 
                &\leftarrow \alpha \log\left( 
                        \frac{1}{\alpha} 
                            \left( \frac{D-i}{D} + \Vments(\schain{i+1}) \right)\right) \\
            & \leq \max\left(
                \frac{D-i}{D},\Vments(\schain{i+1})\right) 
                    + \alphaments\log(2) \label{mbm:one} \\
            & \leq \max\left(
                \frac{D-i}{D},\Vments(\schain{i+1})\right) + \frac{1}{D}
        \end{align}
        %
        where inequality (\ref{mbm:one}) uses the property of log-sum-exp that $\alpha \log \sum_{j=1}^\ell \exp (x_i/\alpha) \leq \max_j (x_j) + \alpha \log(\ell)$. 
        
        Then, by induction on $i=D, D-1, \ldots, 1$, it holds that $\Vments(\schain{i})\leq \frac{D-(i-1)}{D}$:
        \begin{align}
            \Vments(\schain{i}) 
                &\leq \max\left(
                    \frac{D-i}{D},\Vments(\schain{i+1})\right) + \frac{1}{D} \\
                &= \frac{D-i}{D} + \frac{1}{D} \\
                &= \frac{D-(i-1)}{D}.
        \end{align}

        Hence, it must be the case that $\Qments(\schain{i},\aL) > \Qments(\schain{i},\aR)$ and $\piments(\aR|\schain{i}) \leq \frac{1}{2}$ for all $1\leq i\leq D$.
        
        Let $Y(n)$ be the event that the state $\schain{E}$ is visited in the $n$th trial. Additionally, let $Z(n)$ be the event that $\schain{E}$ has not been visited on any of the first $n$ trials. That is, $Z(n)=\bigcap_{j=1}^n \neg Y(j)$. Additionally note that $Z(n)$ implies that $\Vments(\schain{E}) = 0$ and so:
        \begin{align}
            \Pr(Y(n) | Z(n-1)) 
                &= \Pr(Y(n) | \Vments(\schain{E}) = 0)  \\
                &\leq 2^{-D}.
        \end{align}

        Hence, the probability that $\schain{E}$ is not visited in the first $n$ trials can be bounded below inductively:
        \begin{align}
            \Pr(Z(n)) 
                &= \Pr\left(\neg Y(n) \cap Z(n-1)\right) \\
                &= \Pr\left(\neg Y(n) \middle| Z(n-1)\right) \Pr(Z(n-1)) \\
                &\geq (1-2^{-D}) \Pr(Z(n-1)) \\
                &\geq \ldots \\
                &\geq (1-2^{-D})^n \Pr(Z(0)) \\
                &\geq (1-n2^{-D}), \label{mbm:two}
        \end{align}
        %
        where the last line uses Bernoulli's inequality and $\Pr(Z(0))=1$. $Z(0)$ is trivially true, as no trials have been run yet.
        
        From the argument above, $Z(n)$ implies that $\Qments(\schain{1},\aR) \leq \frac{D-1}{D} = \Qments(\schain{1},\aL)$ and so $\psiments(\schain{1}) = \aL \neq \aR = \pi^*(\schain{1})$. Hence, $\Pr(\psiments\neq\pi^*) = \Pr(Z(n)) \geq (1-n2^{-D})$, which is greater than $\frac{1}{2}$ if $n\leq 2^{D-1}$.
        
        For the second part of the result, a simple regret of at least $\frac{D-1}{D}$ is incurred with probability at least $\Pr(Z(n))$. Hence,  the expected simple regret can be bounded below as follows:
        \begin{align}
            \mathbb{E}\left[\sreg(\schain{1},\psiments)\right] 
                & \geq \left(1-\frac{D-1}{D}\right) \Pr(Z(n)) \\
                & \geq \frac{1}{D} \left( 1-n2^{-D} \right).
        \end{align}
        
        Then $|\cl{S}|=4D+3 \leq 5D$ for $D\geq 3$ and so substituting $D\geq \frac{|\cl{S}|}{5}$ gives the result with $c=\frac{1}{D}$ and $k=2^{1/5}$:
        \begin{align}
            \mathbb{E}\left[\sreg(\schain{1},\psiments)\right]
                &\geq \frac{1}{D} \left( 1-n2^{-D} \right) \\
                &\geq \frac{1}{D} \left( 1-\frac{n}{\left(2^{\frac{1}{5}}\right)^{|\cl{S}|}} \right).
        \end{align}
    \end{proofoutline}
